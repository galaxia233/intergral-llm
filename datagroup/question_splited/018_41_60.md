41.60 Is it possible to define $f(x, y) = \frac{x^3 + y^3}{x^2 + y^2}$ at (0, 0) so that $f(x, y)$ is continuous?

Note that

$$
\left| \frac {x ^ {3} + y ^ {3}}{x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}} \right| \leq \frac {\left| x ^ {3} \right|}{x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}} + \frac {\left| y ^ {3} \right|}{x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}} = \frac {x ^ {2}}{x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}} | x | + \frac {y ^ {2}}{x ^ {2} + y ^ {2}} | y | \leq | x | + | y | \rightarrow 0 \quad \text {a s} (x, y) \rightarrow (0, 0)
$$

So, $\lim_{(x,y)\to (0,0)}\frac{x^3 + y^3}{x^2 + y^2} = 0$ . Hence, if we define $f(0,0) = 0$ , then $f(x,y)$ will be continuous, since it is obvious that $f(x,y)$ is continuous at all points different from the origin.
