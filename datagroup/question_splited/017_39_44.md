39.44 Prove that $e$ is irrational.

$e^{x} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{n}}{n!}$ . Hence, $\frac{1}{e} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}$ . By the alternating series theorem, $0 < \left|\frac{1}{e} - \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}\right| < \frac{1}{k!}$ . Therefore, $0 < (k-1)! \left|\frac{1}{e} - \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}\right| < \frac{1}{k} \leq \frac{1}{2}$ for $k \geq 2$ . So $0 < \left|\frac{(k-1)!}{e} - (k-1)! \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}\right| < \frac{1}{2}$ . But $(k-1)! \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}$ is an integer. If $e$ were rational, then $k$ could be chosen large enough so that $(k-1)! / e$ would be an integer. Hence, for $k$ large enough and suitably chosen, $\left|\frac{(k-1)!}{e} - (k-1)! \sum_{n=0}^{k-1} \frac{(-1)^{n}}{n!}\right|$ would be an integer strictly between 0 and $\frac{1}{2}$ , which is impossible.
