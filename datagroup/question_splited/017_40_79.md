40.79 Apply the method of Problem 40.78 to Problem 40.77.

The line is $x = 3 + t$ , $y = 2 + t$ , $z = 1 + t$ . $P_0$ is $(1, 2, 1)$ . Let $\vec{R} = (3, 2, 1)$ and $Q = (4, 3, 2)$ . $\overrightarrow{RQ}$ is $(1, 1, 1)$ , $\overrightarrow{P_0R}$ is $(2, 0, 0)$ , and $\overrightarrow{P_0Q} = (3, 1, 1)$ . Then $A = \overrightarrow{P_0R} \times \overrightarrow{P_0Q} = (0, -2, 2)$ , and $A \times \overrightarrow{RQ} = (0, -2, 2) \times (1, 1, 1) = (-4, 2, 2)$ . So, the line $P_0P_1$ is $x = 1 - 4s$ , $y = 2 + 2s$ , $z = 1 + 2s$ . To get the intersection of line $P_0P_1$ with the given line, equate the $x$ -coordinates: $3 + t = 1 - 4s$ . Then $t = -2 - 4s$ . Substitute in the $y$ -coordinates: $2 + (-2 - 4s) = 2 + 2s$ . So, $s = -\frac{1}{3}$ . Hence, $t = -\frac{2}{3}$ . Note that the $z$ -coordinates then agree: $1 - \frac{2}{3} = 1 + 2(-\frac{1}{3})$ . The intersection point $P_1$ is $x = \frac{7}{3}$ , $y = \frac{4}{3}$ , $z = \frac{1}{3}$ . The distance between $P_0$ and the given line is $\overline{P_0P_1} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{4}{3}\right)^2 + \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)^2 + \left(-\frac{2}{3}\right)^2} = \sqrt{\frac{8}{3}}$ . These are the same results that were found in Problem 40.77.
