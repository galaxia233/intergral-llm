43.66 Use Lagrange multipliers to maximize $x_{1}y_{1} + \dots + x_{n}y_{n}$ subject to the constraints $\sum_{i=1}^{n}x_{i}^{2} = 1$ and $\sum_{i=1}^{n}y_{i}^{2} = 1$ .

It is obvious that we can restrict our attention to nonnegative numbers. We must maximize $f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n},y_{1},\ldots ,y_{n}) = x_{1}y_{1} + \dots +x_{n}y_{n}\quad \text{subject to the constraints}\quad g(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}) = \left(\sum_{i = 1}^{n}x_{1}^{2}\right) - 1 = 0$ and $h(y_1,\ldots ,y_n) = \left(\sum y_1^2\right) - 1 = 0.\quad \nabla f = (y_1,y_2,\ldots ,y_n,x_1,\ldots ,x_n),\quad \nabla g = (2x_1,\ldots ,2x_n,0,\ldots ,0),$ $\nabla h = (0,\dots ,0,2y_1,\dots ,2y_n).$ Let $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g + \mu \nabla h.$ Then $(y_{1},\ldots ,y_{n},x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}) = \lambda (2x_{1},\ldots ,2x_{n},$ $0,\dots ,0) + \mu (0,\dots ,0,2y_1,\dots ,2y_n).$ Hence, $y_{1} = 2\lambda x_{1},\ldots ,y_{n} = 2\lambda x_{n},\quad x_{1} = 2\mu y_{1},\ldots ,x_{n} = 2\mu y_{n}.$ Clearly, $\lambda \neq 0.$ (Otherwise, $y_{1} = \dots = y_{n} = 0,$ contradicting $\sum_{i = 1}^{n}y_i^2 = 1.)$ Similarly, $\mu \neq 0.$ Now, $1 = \sum_{i = 1}^{n}y_{i}^{2} = \sum_{i = 1}^{n}4\lambda^{2}x_{i}^{2} = 4\lambda^{2}\sum_{i = 1}^{n}x_{i}^{2} = 4\lambda^{2}.$ Hence, $\lambda^2 = \frac{1}{4}$ and $\lambda = \pm \frac{1}{2}.$ Since $y_{i} = 2\lambda x_{i}$ and $x_{i}\geq 0$ and $y_{i}\geq 0, \lambda = \frac{1}{2}.$ Similarly, $\mu = \frac{1}{2}.$ Hence, $y_{i} = x_{i}.$ Therefore, $x_{1}y_{1} + \dots +x_{n}y_{n} = x_{1}^{2} + \dots +x_{n}^{2} = 1.$ Hence, the maximum value is 1. [This result becomes obvious when the problem is restated as: Maximize the dot product of two unit vectors.]
