39.11 If $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (x - a)^n$ for $|x - a| < r$ , prove that $a_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}$ . In other words, if $f(x)$ has a power series expansion about $a$ , that power series must be the Taylor series for $f(x)$ about $a$ .

$f(a) = a_0$ . It can be shown that the power series converges uniformly on $|x - a| \leq \rho < r$ , allowing differentiation term by term: $f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n a_n (x - a)^{n-1}$ , $f''(x) = \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n (n-1) a_n (x - a)^{n-2}, \ldots$ , $f^{(k)}(x) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} n (n-1) \cdots [n-(k-1)] a_n (x - a)^{n-k}$ . If we let $x = a$ , $f^{(k)}(a) = k (k-1) \cdots 1 \cdot a_k = k! \cdot a_k$ . Hence, $a_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}$ .
