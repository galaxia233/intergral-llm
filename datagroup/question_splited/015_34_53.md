34.95 For a circle of radius $a$ , traced out in the counterclockwise direction, show that the curvature is $1 / a$ , and the radius of curvature is $a$ , the radius of the circle.

If $(x_0, y_0)$ is the center of the circle, then $\mathbf{R}(t) = (x_0 + a\cos t, y_0 + a\sin t)$ traces out the circle, where $t$ is the angle from the positive $x$ -axis to $\mathbf{R}(t) - (x_0, y_0)$ . Then $\mathbf{R}'(t) = a(-\sin t, \cos t)$ , and $ds/dt = a$ . The angle $\phi$ made by the positive $x$ -axis with $\mathbf{R}'(t)$ is $\tan^{-1}\left(\frac{a\cos t}{-a\sin t}\right) = \tan^{-1}(-\cot t)$ , or that angle $+\pi$ . Hence,

$$
\frac {d \phi}{d s} = \frac {1}{1 + \cot^ {2} t} (\csc^ {2} t) \cdot \frac {d t}{d s} = \frac {d t}{d s} = \frac {1}{a}
$$

So, $\kappa = 1 / a$ , and by definition, the radius of curvature is $a$ .
