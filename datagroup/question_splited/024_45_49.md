45.49 Gauss' theorem, $\iint_{\mathcal{C}} \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n} dS = \iiint_{\mathcal{R}} \operatorname{div} \mathbf{F} dV$ , where $\mathcal{C}$ is a closed surface bounding a three-dimensional region $\mathcal{R}$ , is the analogue to Green's theorem in the plane. Verify Gauss' theorem for the surface $\mathcal{C}$ : $x^{2} + y^{2} + z^{2} = a^{2}$ and the vector field $\mathbf{F} = (x, y, z)$ .

We have $\operatorname{div} \mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3$ and, on $\mathcal{C}$ , $\mathbf{F} = a\mathbf{n}$ . Hence, the theorem reads

$$
\iint_ {\mathcal {C}} a d S = \iiint_ {\mathcal {R}} 3 d V \quad \text {o r} \quad V = \frac {1}{3} a S
$$

which is the correct equation $\frac{4}{3}\pi a^3 = \frac{1}{3} a(4\pi a^2)$ .
