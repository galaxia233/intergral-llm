34.68 If $\mathbf{F}(u) = \mathbf{A}$ for all $u$ , show that $\mathbf{F}'(u) = \mathbf{0}$ , and, conversely, if $\mathbf{F}'(u) = \mathbf{0}$ for all $u$ , then $\mathbf{F}(u)$ is a constant vector.

Let $\mathbf{F}(u) = (f(u), g(u))$ . If $f(u) = a_{1}$ and $g(u) = a_{2}$ for all $u$ , then $\mathbf{F}'(u) = (f'(u), g'(u)) = (0, 0) = \mathbf{0}$ . Conversely, if $\mathbf{F}'(u) = \mathbf{0}$ for all $u$ , then $f'(u) = 0$ and $g'(u) = 0$ for all $u$ , and, therefore, $f(u)$ and $g(u)$ are constants, and, thus, $\mathbf{F}(u)$ is constant.
