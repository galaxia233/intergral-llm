32.60 If $L\{f\}$ and $L\{f'\}$ are defined, show that $L\{f'\} = -f(0) + sL\{f\}$ .

For $L\{f^{\prime}\}$ , we use integration by parts with $u = e^{-st}$ , $dv = f'(t)dt$ . Then $L\{f'\} = \int_{0}^{\infty}e^{-st}f'(t)dt = \lim_{v\to +\infty}[e^{-st}f(t)]_0^v +s\int_0^v e^{-st}f(t)dt] = \lim_{v\to +\infty}[e^{-sv}f(v) - f(0) + s\int_0^v e^{-st}f(t)dt] = -f(0) + sL\{f\}$ . [Here, we have used the basic hypothesis that $\lim_{v\to +\infty}e^{-sv}f(v) = 0$ .]
