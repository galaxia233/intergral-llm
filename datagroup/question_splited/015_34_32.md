34.74 Assume that an object moves on a circle of radius $r$ with constant speed $v > 0$ . Show that the acceleration vector is directed toward the center of the circle and has length $v^2 / r$ .

The position vector $\mathbf{R}(t)$ satisfies $|\mathbf{R}(t)| = r$ and $|\mathbf{R}'(t)| = v$ . Let $\theta$ be the angle from the positive $x$ -axis to $\mathbf{R}(t)$ and let $s$ be the corresponding arc length on the circle. Then $s = r\theta$ , and, since the object moves with constant speed $v$ , $s = vt$ . Hence, $\theta = vt / r$ . We can write $\mathbf{R}(t) = r(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)$ . By the chain rule, $\mathbf{R}'(t) = r(-\sin \theta, \cos \theta)\frac{d\theta}{dt} = r(-\sin \theta, \cos \theta)\frac{v}{r} = v(-\sin \theta, \cos \theta)$ . Again by the chain rule, $\mathbf{R}''(t) = v(-\cos \theta - \sin \theta)\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -v(\cos \theta, \sin \theta)\frac{v}{r} = -\frac{v^2}{r^2} (r\cos \theta, r\sin \theta) = -\frac{v^2}{r^2}\mathbf{R}(t)$ . Hence, the acceleration vector $\mathbf{R}''(t)$ points in the opposite direction to $\mathbf{R}(t)$ , that is, toward the center of the circle, and $|\mathbf{R}''(t)| = \frac{v^2}{r^2} |\mathbf{R}(t)| = \frac{v^2}{r^2} \cdot r = v^2 / r$ .
